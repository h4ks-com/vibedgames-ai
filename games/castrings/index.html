Steerable gliders for language — a concrete demo

Every character steers a single glider a character-specific direction (using the paper's direction law). A word therefore becomes a controlled trajectory through the cellular-automaton medium — a deterministic, order-sensitive signature of the string. Type two words and compare.

What is this actually good for? (for the linguist in the room)

  1. A transparent text-feature extractor for the digital humanities. The trajectory is a reproducible geometric fingerprint of a string that — unlike letter-frequency “bags” — is sensitive to order. Such fingerprints are usable features for clustering manuscripts, flagging orthographic/scribal variation, screening authorship, or measuring string similarity — all GPU-free and fully inspectable.
  2. A teaching instrument. A vivid, mechanistic picture of how a dynamical system can represent and tell apart linguistic sequences: word order, minimal pairs, phonotactics.
  3. Reservoir computing on text (an established niche): tokens become controllable information carriers; the steering law gives interpretable encoding instead of a black box.
Honest scope: this is a research instrument and an alternative, transparent encoding — not a replacement for statistical NLP. Its value is determinism, interpretability, and the controllable physics, demonstrated below: the same string always yields the same signature, anagrams yield different signatures (order is encoded), and related strings yield related ones.

Each letter a–z (and other characters) maps to a heading; the glider is re-steered to that heading for a few ticks per character via “glider surgery” (editing 18 of the rule’s 16,384 entries). The drawn curve is the glider’s centre-of-mass path. Similarity = 1 − normalised RMS distance between the two resampled, registered trajectories.
