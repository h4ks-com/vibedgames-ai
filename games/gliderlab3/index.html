Why should a linguist (or anyone into BERT / GPT / LLMs) care?

Large language models do one thing over and over: they move and mix information between word-positions. That’s what “attention” is — a mechanism for routing a piece of information from one token to another and combining it with others. Strip away the mathematics and the core operation is just: carry a signal somewhere, and let signals interact.

  • A glider is the simplest tangible picture of “a piece of information that travels.” Here you can aim it (steer its direction), route it along a path, and make two of them collide to combine — the same three primitives (transport, routing, interaction) that, scaled up and made learnable, power a transformer.
  • The “Word → Glider” tab makes it physical. A word becomes a steered trajectory. You can see that order matters (anagrams trace different paths) and that similar words trace similar paths — the intuition that sequences carry structure, not just a bag of letters.
  • Why it’s worth studying even unfinished: this is a fully transparent, GPU-free, deterministic medium where every step is inspectable. It’s a way to build intuition about how information can be transported and transformed — and a sandbox for “unconventional computing” where the rules themselves come from fractals.

Honest scope. Steerable gliders are not a replacement for GPT or BERT, and we’ve tested that they don’t beat standard text tools at real tasks. Their value here is intuition, transparency, and the underlying physics — a hands-on way to grasp the idea of “routing information”, which is the heart of modern language models, without a single equation.
